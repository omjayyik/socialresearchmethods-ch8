Chapter 8: Sampling

Selecting a Few to Understand the Many

Why Not Study Everyone?

Sampling is the process of selecting a small number of cases from a larger group. We do this because it's often impossible, impractical, or too expensive to study an entire population. A carefully selected sample can provide a surprisingly accurate picture of the whole group.

The Two Paths of Sampling

Your research goal determines your sampling strategy. Are you trying to generalize findings to a large population, or are you exploring a specific group in-depth?

Nonprobability Sampling

Used when it's difficult or unnecessary to create a precise, representative sample. Common in qualitative research where the goal is deep understanding, not generalization.

Convenience Sampling

Selecting cases that are easy to reach (e.g., interviewing shoppers at a mall). It's fast and cheap, but can be highly unrepresentative.

Quota Sampling

A step up from convenience. You first identify relevant categories (e.g., age, gender) and then select a certain number of cases to fill each "quota."

Purposive Sampling

Using expert judgment to select cases that are most relevant to the research question (e.g., studying a specific social movement by interviewing its leaders).

Snowball Sampling

Starting with one or two members of a hard-to-reach population and asking them to refer you to others (e.g., studying undocumented immigrants or members of a secret society).

Probability Sampling

The gold standard for quantitative research. Its key feature is **random selection**, where every case in the population has an equal chance of being chosen. This allows us to generalize our findings from the sample to the population with a known degree of accuracy.

Simple Random Sample (SRS)

The most basic method. You create a list of everyone in the population (a sampling frame) and randomly select cases, like drawing names from a hat.

Systematic Sampling

Simpler than SRS. You calculate a sampling interval (e.g., every 10th person) and select cases at that interval from your sampling frame.

Stratified Sampling

First, divide the population into subgroups ("strata") based on a key characteristic (e.g., year in college). Then, draw a random sample from *each* subgroup. This ensures representation.

Cluster Sampling

For large, dispersed populations. You first randomly sample larger units ("clusters," like cities), then randomly sample smaller units within those clusters (like neighborhoods), and so on, until you reach individuals.

The Power of Randomness

Probability sampling works because of a powerful statistical idea: the **Central Limit Theorem**. It states that if you draw many random samples, the results of those samples will form a perfect bell-shaped curve around the true population value.

1. The Population

Imagine a large population. We want to know its true average (the parameter), but we can't measure everyone.

2. Many Random Samples

So, we take many random samples. Each sample gives us its own estimate (a statistic). Some will be high, some low.

3. The Sampling Distribution

When we plot the results of all our samples, they form a bell curve centered exactly on the true population parameter!

The Takeaway

This tells us that while any single sample might be slightly off, the laws of probability ensure that it's most likely to be very close to the true value. This is what allows us to be confident in our results.

How Big Should My Sample Be?

This is one of the most common questions in research. The answer is not a simple percentage (like "10% of the population"). It depends on several factors:

1. Desired Accuracy

How small do you want your margin of error to be? More accuracy requires a larger sample.

2. Population Variation

How diverse is your population? A more varied population requires a larger sample to capture that diversity.

3. Number of Variables

How many different things are you studying at once? Analyzing many subgroups requires a larger sample.

A key insight: for large populations (e.g., over 100,000), the sample size needed for a given level of accuracy stays relatively constant. A sample of ~1,500 can represent the entire United States with a small margin of error!
